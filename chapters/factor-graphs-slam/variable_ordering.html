Variable Ordering and COLAMD

1 Variable Ordering

The natural question after seeing fill-ins is: which variable should we eliminate first? Getting this order right turns out to matter a great deal for performance.

1.1 Neighbours

Suppose the graph looks like:

\[ x_1 - x_2 - x_3 \]

The neighbours of \(x_2\) are \(x_1\) and \(x_3\):

\[ N(x_2) = \{x_1, x_3\} \]

1.2 Elimination Rule

When eliminating a variable, connect all of its neighbours together.

So after eliminating \(x_2\) from the graph above, the result is:

\[ x_1 - x_3 \]

with a new edge appearing directly between \(x_1\) and \(x_3\) — this is exactly the fill-in from before.

A bigger example: suppose \(N(x_1) = \{x_2, x_3, x_4\}\), arranged as:

\[ \begin{array}{c} x_2 \\ | \\ x_3 - x_1 - x_4 \end{array} \]

Eliminating \(x_1\) connects all three neighbours together:

\[ \begin{array}{c} x_2 \\ / \; \backslash \\ x_3 - x_4 \end{array} \]

1.3 Clique

A clique is a set of vertices where every pair is connected. Formally, for \(C = \{v_1, v_2, \dots, v_k\}\):

\[ C \text{ is a clique if } (v_i, v_j) \in E \; \forall\, i \neq j \]

1.4 The Fundamental Theorem of Elimination

When eliminating a variable, its neighbours become a clique.

This single fact is the root cause of fill-ins — every elimination step forces all of the eliminated variable’s neighbours to become fully interconnected.

1.5 Why Are Cliques Bad?

Here’s a concrete example. Suppose \(x\) has four neighbours \(n_1, n_2, n_3, n_4\):

\[ \begin{array}{c} n_2 \\ | \\ n_1 - x - n_3 \\ | \\ n_4 \end{array} \quad (4 \text{ edges}) \]

Eliminating \(x\) turns \(\{n_1, n_2, n_3, n_4\}\) into a clique — every pair now connected:

\[ \binom{4}{2} = 6 \text{ edges} \]

The graph became denser. In general, if a variable has \(k\) neighbours, eliminating it creates:

ImportantFill-in Growth Is Quadratic

\[ \binom{k}{2} = \frac{k(k-1)}{2} \text{ edges} \]

The growth is quadratic in \(k\), which escalates fast:

  • 2 neighbours → 1 edge
  • 100 neighbours → 4950 edges → computational disaster

We should eliminate variables with smaller degree first — not because the math changes, but purely as a computational trick to keep the graph as sparse as possible for as long as possible.

2 Minimum Degree Ordering

We’ve established that eliminating low-degree variables first keeps fill-in under control — this strategy is called Minimum Degree Ordering. But there’s a catch: choosing the elimination order is itself an NP-hard problem. We can’t just try every possible ordering and pick the best one — there are \(n!\) possibilities.

Worse, since removing one variable changes the graph, a naive algorithm would need to recompute degrees after every single elimination. That’s very expensive at scale.

2.1 Approximate Minimum Degree (AMD)

The practical fix: don’t compute the exact degree every time. Instead, estimate how many neighbours a node is likely to have after fill-in, and use that estimate to guide the ordering.

AMD doesn’t always produce the optimal ordering — but it produces a good one, cheaply enough to actually be usable on large problems.

3 COLAMD: Column Approximate Minimum Degree

COLAMD takes this a step further with an important shift in perspective: it optimizes the ordering on the Jacobian, not the Hessian.

This matters because the Hessian \(H\) can already have fill-ins baked into it — working directly from the Jacobian avoids inheriting that problem.

3.1 Why “Column”?

Recall the structure of the Jacobian: every factor is a row, and every variable is a column. COLAMD works by reordering the columns — i.e., reordering the variables. Critically, this reordering never changes the underlying math, only the order in which variables get eliminated.

3.2 How It Works

Suppose we have a Jacobian \(J\) whose columns correspond to variables \([x_1, x_2, x_3, l_1]^T\):

\[ J = \begin{bmatrix} * & * & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & * & * & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & * & * \\ * & 0 & 0 & * \end{bmatrix} \]

Step 1: Convert the Jacobian to a bipartite graph.

This is a graph with two types of nodes — rows are one type of node, columns are another. Each factor row connects to the columns (variables) it involves:

\[ f_1 \to \{x_1, x_2\}, \quad f_2 \to \{x_2, x_3\}, \quad f_3 \to \{x_3, l_1\}, \quad f_4 \to \{x_1, l_1\} \]

Step 2: Compute column degree — how many other columns share a row with this one.

For example, column \(x_1\) appears in rows \(f_1\) and \(f_4\). Row \(f_1\) also contains \(x_2\), and row \(f_4\) also contains \(l_1\). So \(x_1\) is related to \(\{x_2, l_1\}\):

\[ \therefore \; \text{approximate degree of } x_1 = 2 \]

This column-degree estimate is exactly the quantity COLAMD uses to decide which variable to eliminate first — lower approximate degree means eliminating that variable is less likely to trigger a cascade of expensive fill-ins.
