Cholesky & QR

1 Cholesky Factorization

We’re left needing to solve the normal equations:

\[ A^T A \, \delta = A^T b \]

Instead of inverting \(A^T A\) directly (expensive and numerically fragile), we factor it:

\[ A^T A = R^T R \quad \to \; \text{Cholesky factor} \]

where \(R\) and \(R^T\) are triangular matrices. Triangular systems are cheap to solve — that’s the whole motivation here.

Substituting back into the normal equations:

\[ R^T R \, \delta = A^T b \]

Rather than solving this all at once, we split it into two much easier triangular systems:

  1. Solve for \(y\) using \(R^T y = A^T b\)
  2. Then solve for \(\delta\) using \(R\delta = y\)

Each of these is a simple triangular solve — no expensive inversion required.

TipThe payoff

Cholesky takes the computation from \(O(n^3)\) (a direct inversion) down to \(O(n^2)\) per solve, by replacing one hard problem with two cheap triangular ones.

2 QR Factorization

Cholesky factors \(A^T A\). QR takes a different approach — it factors \(A\) itself:

\[ A = QR \]

where \(Q\) is orthogonal and \(R\) is upper triangular.

Orthogonal means \(Q^T Q = I\), which immediately gives us \(Q^{-1} = Q^T\) — the inverse of \(Q\) is just its transpose, essentially free to compute.

2.1 Deriving the Simplified System

Starting from the original system \(A\delta = b\), substitute \(A = QR\):

\[ QR\delta = b \]

Multiply both sides by \(Q^T\):

\[ Q^T QR\delta = Q^T b \]

Since \(Q^T Q = I\), this collapses to:

\[ R\delta = Q^T b \]

This is much simpler to solve — \(R\) is triangular, so it’s just a back-substitution away from \(\delta\).

3 Cholesky vs. QR: Same Goal, Different Routes

Both methods are ultimately solving the same problem, just via different factorizations:

Method Route Ever forms \(A^T A\)?
Cholesky \(A \to A^T A \to R^T R \to\) solve Yes
QR \(A \to QR \to\) solve No

3.1 Trade-offs

Cholesky QR
Speed Faster, especially on sparse matrices Slower
Numerical stability More sensitive to error More stable
Typical use Default choice for SLAM (systems are almost always sparse) Preferred when stability matters more than speed
NoteWhy QR is more stable

The gap comes down to what happens to small errors: if \(A\) is only slightly inaccurate, computing \(A^T A\) roughly squares that inaccuracy. QR avoids this entirely, since it never forms \(A^T A\) in the first place.

The practical upshot: QR is more numerically stable but slower, while Cholesky is faster — especially on sparse problems — but slightly more sensitive to numerical error. Which one gets used often comes down to this trade-off between speed and stability for the specific problem at hand.

4 Optimization Algorithms Only Differ in One Thing

Zooming out from Cholesky and QR — which are both about solving one linear system — it’s worth placing that step inside the bigger picture of how the optimizer decides to move at all.

Optimization algorithms only differ in how they decide to take the next step. Everything else — the problem setup, the goal — stays the same.

The three methods worth knowing are:

  1. Steepest Descent
  2. Gauss-Newton
  3. Levenberg-Marquardt

4.1 The Optimization Problem

All three algorithms are solving the exact same underlying problem. Suppose we have an objective function:

\[ J(x) : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R} \]

where \(x\) is an \(n\)-dimensional vector, and \(J(x)\) is a scalar cost.

The goal:

\[ x^{*} = \operatorname*{argmin}_{x} J(x) \]

In SLAM specifically, this cost is the sum of squared, whitened residuals we derived earlier:

\[ J(x) = \sum_i \| r_i(x) \|^2_{\Sigma_i}, \qquad r_i(x) = z_i - h_i(x_i) \]

Every algorithm below is just a different strategy for minimizing this same \(J(x)\).

5 First Order vs. Second Order Optimization

Every optimization algorithm tries to approximate what \(J(x+\delta)\) looks like near the current estimate \(x\) — essentially asking “if I step in direction \(\delta\), how does the cost change?” There are two common ways to approximate this.

5.1 1st Order: Gradient Only

\[ J(x+\delta) \approx J(x) + \nabla J(x)^T \delta \]

Only the gradient is used here — this tells us the direction of steepest change, but nothing about how the cost curves.

5.2 2nd Order: Gradient and Curvature

\[ J(x+\delta) \approx J(x) + \nabla J(x)^T \delta + \frac{1}{2}\delta^T H \delta \]

where \(H = \nabla^2 J(x)\) is the Hessian matrix — it captures curvature, i.e., how the gradient itself is changing.

TipWhy curvature helps

Knowing curvature lets an algorithm make a much better-informed guess about how big a step to take — instead of just which direction to move in.

6 Comparing the Algorithms

The differences between these methods come down to whether — and how — they use the Hessian:

Algorithm Uses gradient Uses Hessian
Gradient Descent ✓ ✗
Gauss-Newton ✓ Approximate
Newton ✓ Exact
Levenberg-Marquardt ✓ Approximate + Damping

A few things worth noticing in this table:

  • Gradient descent is the cheapest per step, since it ignores curvature entirely — but that also makes it the least informed about how far to step, often leading to slow zig-zagging convergence.
  • Newton’s method uses the true Hessian and converges fastest in principle, but computing the exact Hessian is often expensive or impractical for large problems like SLAM.
  • Gauss-Newton is the practical middle ground: it approximates the Hessian as \(H \approx A^T A\) (as we derived in the normal equations), avoiding the cost of computing second derivatives directly. This is exactly the \(A^T A\) we’ve been factoring with Cholesky and QR above.
  • Levenberg-Marquardt builds on Gauss-Newton by adding a damping term, blending it with gradient descent behavior when the Gauss-Newton step would be unreliable. This is what makes LM more robust in practice, particularly when far from the optimum.
NoteThe throughline

This progression — from no curvature information, to approximate curvature, to approximate-with-safety-net — is really what connects all three algorithms back to the same Taylor expansion framework.
