Sparsity and Fill-ins

1 Sparsity: Why SLAM Scales

This is the reason optimizing thousands of poses doesn’t take hours: the Hessian matrix contains mostly zeros.

SLAM naturally produces sparse matrices because each pose \(x_i\) only depends on other poses in its immediate locality — nearby poses in the trajectory or landmarks it directly observed, not the entire state.

1.1 Where the Sparsity Comes From

Suppose the residual for one factor is:

\[ r_i(x) = z_i - h_i(x) \]

where \(x\) is the complete state vector:

\[ x = \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \\ x_3 \\ l_1 \\ l_2 \end{bmatrix} \]

Now suppose \(r_i\) only actually depends on \(x_2\) and \(l_1\) — a completely reasonable scenario, since a single factor (like one landmark observation) only involves a couple of variables:

\[ r_i = r_i(x_2, l_1) \]

The Jacobian \(J_i = \dfrac{\partial r_i}{\partial x}\) has one column per variable in \(x\) — five columns here. But since \(r_i\) doesn’t depend on \(x_1\), \(x_3\), or \(l_2\) at all:

\[ \frac{\partial r_i}{\partial x_1} = 0, \qquad \frac{\partial r_i}{\partial x_3} = \frac{\partial r_i}{\partial l_2} = 0 \]

Every factor produces one sparse Jacobian row — mostly zeros, with nonzero entries only in the columns corresponding to the handful of variables that factor actually touches.

Recall that the approximate Hessian is built directly from these Jacobians, summed across all factors:

\[ H = \sum_i J_i^T \Sigma_i^{-1} J_i \]

so this sparsity in each \(J_i\) propagates directly into \(H\): the Hessian is sparse too. This is precisely why sparse solvers like Cholesky and QR — exploiting this structure — are what make large-scale SLAM computationally feasible in the first place.

2 Sparse Isn’t the Whole Story

Simply having a sparse Hessian is not enough on its own — the matrix becomes much denser during the factorization step. This surprising fact is worth understanding, since it directly affects how efficient SLAM solvers actually are in practice.

3 Fill-ins During Factorization

If \(H\) is sparse going in, the resulting \(R\) after Cholesky factorization will not stay sparse. New non-zero entries can appear in places that were originally zero — these are called fill-ins.

4 Why Do Fill-ins Appear?

We’re trying to solve the Gauss-Newton update:

\[ H\delta = -g \qquad \Longleftrightarrow \qquad A^T A\, \delta = -A^T b \]

where \(\delta\) is a stacked vector of all the unknowns:

\[ \delta = \begin{bmatrix} \delta_1 \\ \delta_2 \\ \delta_3 \end{bmatrix} \]

One approach would be to solve for every variable simultaneously. But for thousands of variables, this isn’t computationally possible to do directly — it’s simply too large a system to tackle head-on.

4.1 A Familiar Idea: Gauss Elimination

Remember solving small linear systems by elimination? Take:

\[ x + y = 5, \qquad 2x + y = 8 \]

We eliminate \(x\) by expressing it from the first equation, \(x = y - 5\), and substituting into the second: \(2(y-5) + y = 8\) — then solve.

SLAM does the same thing, just at massive scale. Instead of solving 1000 poses all at once, we solve them one by one, by elimination.

5 Where the New Edges Come From

Here’s the key insight, illustrated with a small example. Suppose we have three variables connected like this:

\[ B - M - L \]

Initially, \(B\) knows nothing about \(L\) — there’s no direct edge between them, only an indirect path through \(M\).

Now, if we eliminate \(M\), the information that used to flow through \(M\) needs to be preserved somehow — it can’t just vanish. So a new constraint appears directly between \(B\) and \(L\), and the graph becomes:

\[ B - L \]

NoteWhy It’s Called a “Fill-in”

The new edge fills in a matrix entry that used to be zero — hence fill-in. This same operation goes by another name in estimation theory: the Schur complement.

6 The Bigger Picture

This reframes what elimination is actually doing: when you eliminate a variable, you are not throwing away information. You’re compressing all the information that variable carried into a new constraint between its former neighbors.

That compression is exactly why fill-ins appear — and why, even though SLAM’s Hessian starts out sparse, the matrices that show up partway through solving it can end up considerably denser. Managing this trade-off (choosing the order in which variables get eliminated, to minimize fill-in) turns out to be one of the most important practical concerns in making large-scale SLAM solvers fast.
