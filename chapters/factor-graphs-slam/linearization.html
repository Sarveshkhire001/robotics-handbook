Linearization & Whitening

1 The Iterative Recipe

Solving a non-linear problem numerically comes down to five repeated steps — this is the gist of Gauss-Newton / LM:

  1. Linearize the non-linear residuals
  2. Form a large sparse linear system
  3. Solve for a small update \(\delta\)
  4. Update the state
  5. Repeat until convergence

At a higher level, the whole pipeline looks like:

Non-linear problem → Taylor expansion → linear least squares → normal equations → solve via Cholesky/QR → update state → repeat

The one idea underlying all of it: approximate the non-linear function locally with a straight line, solve that easy linear problem, then re-linearize around the new estimate and repeat. Each iteration gets closer to the true non-linear optimum.

2 Linearizing the Cost Function

2.1 Taylor Expansion, the Engine Behind It All

The linearization step comes straight from a first-order Taylor expansion:

\[ h_i(x_i) = h_i(x_i^0 + \delta_i) \approx h_i(x_i^0) + H_i \delta_i \]

2.2 A Simple (Linear) Warm-Up Example

Before applying this to something genuinely non-linear, it helps to see the mechanics on something simple. Take \(f(x) = x^2\) with a current estimate \(x_0 = 2\):

\[ f(x) = f(x_0) + f'(x_0)(x - x_0) + \dots \quad \text{(ignoring higher-order terms)} \]

\[ f(x) \approx f(x_0) + f'(x_0)(x - x_0) \]

Substituting \(x_0 = 2\), so \(f(x_0) = 4\) and \(f'(x_0) = 4\):

\[ f(x) \approx 4 + 4(x - 2) \]

That’s just the tangent line at \(x_0\) — a linear stand-in for the true curve, valid near \(x_0\).

2.3 The Key Mental Shift: Optimize \(\delta\), Not \(x\)

This is the part that trips people up initially: we don’t optimize \(x\) directly — we optimize \(\delta\), the correction to our current estimate:

\[ \delta = x - x_0 \]

This is easier to optimize because, near \(x_0\), the problem becomes linear in \(\delta\). Once we solve for \(\delta\), we simply update \(x \leftarrow x_0 + \delta\) and repeat the whole process at the new estimate.

2.4 The Jacobian

The matrix \(H_i\) appearing in the linearization is the Jacobian:

\[ H_i = \frac{\partial h_i}{\partial x_i} \; \text{evaluated at } x_i^0 \]

TipIntuition

Jacobian = a local sensitivity of the measurement to each unknown variable. Each entry of \(H_i\) just answers: if I nudge this one unknown a tiny bit, how much does the predicted measurement move?

Worked example: suppose the unknowns are a pose and a landmark position:

\[ x = \begin{bmatrix} x_P \\ y_P \\ \theta_P \\ x_l \\ y_l \end{bmatrix} \qquad h(P, l) = \operatorname{atan2}(y_l - y_P,\; x_l - x_P) \]

Then the Jacobian is a row vector, one partial derivative per unknown:

\[ H = \begin{bmatrix} \dfrac{\partial h}{\partial x_P} & \dfrac{\partial h}{\partial y_P} & \dfrac{\partial h}{\partial \theta_P} & \dfrac{\partial h}{\partial x_l} & \dfrac{\partial h}{\partial y_l} \end{bmatrix} \]

Each column corresponds to one equation’s sensitivity to one unknown — stack these rows across all measurements and you get the full Jacobian matrix used in the linear system each iteration solves.

2.5 Substituting the Linearization Back In

Plugging the Taylor expansion back into the least squares cost:

\[ \sum_i \| z_i - h_i(x_i) \|^2 = \sum_i \| z_i - h_i(x_i^0) - H_i \delta_i \|^2 \]

The problem is now linear. This is the whole point of linearizing — the unknown \(\delta\) only appears inside the term \(H_i \delta_i\), and that’s exactly the form (\(H\delta\)) that linear algebra knows how to solve directly: a vector/matrix product.

3 Whitening the Residuals

Right now each factor’s cost still carries its covariance-weighted norm:

\[ \|e\|^2_\Sigma = e^T \Sigma^{-1} e \]

The idea behind whitening is to fold that covariance weighting directly into the residual, so we’re left with an ordinary (unweighted) squared norm — the standard form every least-squares solver expects.

Splitting \(\Sigma^{-1}\) into two symmetric square-root factors:

\[ e^T \Sigma^{-1} e = e^T \left(\Sigma^{-\frac{1}{2}}\right)^T \Sigma^{-\frac{1}{2}} e = \left(\Sigma^{-\frac{1}{2}} e\right)^T \left(\Sigma^{-\frac{1}{2}} e\right) \]

\[ \therefore \; \|e\|^2_\Sigma = \left\| \Sigma^{-\frac{1}{2}} e \right\|^2 \]

In other words: instead of weighting the norm, we can just rescale the residual itself by \(\Sigma^{-\frac{1}{2}}\), and then take a plain Euclidean norm. That rescaling is the “whitening” — it stretches or shrinks each residual dimension according to how much we trust it, so a highly uncertain measurement contributes less to the final cost.

Applying this to our linearized residual:

\[ \left\| \Sigma_i^{-\frac{1}{2}} \left[ z_i - h_i(x_i^0) - H_i \delta_i \right] \right\|^2 \]

3.1 Defining \(A_i\) and \(b_i\)

To tidy this up, we define:

\[ A_i = \Sigma_i^{-\frac{1}{2}} H_i, \qquad b_i = \Sigma_i^{-\frac{1}{2}} \left(z_i - h_i(x_i^0)\right) \]

Term Meaning
\(A_i\) the Jacobian \(H_i\), rescaled by the whitening factor
\(b_i\) the residual at the current estimate, rescaled the same way

4 The Linear System

With that substitution, the whole optimization collapses into a clean, standard linear least squares form:

\[ \delta^{*} = \operatorname*{argmin}_{\delta} \sum_i \| A_i \delta_i - b_i \|^2 \]

Stacking every factor’s \(A_i\) and \(b_i\) together gives one big, sparse linear system:

\[ A\delta = b \]

And solving that system is the actual computational core of every SLAM backend — everything before this was about getting to this system; everything after is about solving it efficiently.

Symbol Name What it is
\(A\) stacked Jacobian every factor’s whitened sensitivity matrix, stacked together
\(b\) stacked residual vector every factor’s whitened residual, stacked together
\(\delta\) state correction what we solve for, then use to update our estimate
NoteWhy “huge” and why “sparse”
  • Huge — stacked across every factor, the system spans every pose and every landmark in the whole trajectory.
  • Sparse — most entries of \(A\) are zero. A factor only touches the handful of variables it directly constrains, so non-zero entries only appear where an actual constraint exists between variables. This is the same sparsity we saw in the factor graph structure — it’s what makes solving a system this large even feasible.

5 Why Not Just Solve \(A\delta = b\) Directly?

It’s tempting to think we’re done once we have the linear system \(A\delta = b\) — just solve it and move on. But there’s a problem:

There is usually no exact solution.

This makes sense once you think about where \(A\) and \(b\) actually come from. Different sensors give different, slightly inconsistent values — no single \(\delta\) satisfies every constraint simultaneously. So instead of solving the system exactly, we find the solution with the smallest total error.

6 Setting Up the Cost

The cost we want to minimize is:

\[ J(\delta) = \|A\delta - b\|^2 \]

Expanding this out:

\[ J(\delta) = (A\delta - b)^T (A\delta - b) = (\delta^T A^T - b^T)(A\delta - b) \]

\[ J(\delta) = \delta^T A^T A \delta - 2\delta^T A^T b + b^T b \]

This is now a quadratic function — the matrix version of the familiar \(ax^2 + bx + c\) from single-variable calculus.

7 Minimizing the Cost

For a quadratic, minimizing is straightforward: take the derivative and set it to zero. Differentiating \(J(\delta)\) with respect to \(\delta\) and setting the result to zero gives:

\[ A^T A \, \delta = A^T b \]

ImportantThe Normal Equations

\[ A^T A \, \delta = A^T b \]

This is the linear system that actually gets solved at every iteration of the optimizer. Everything up to this point — linearization, whitening, stacking factors — exists to produce this one equation.

8 Why “Normal”?

The name isn’t arbitrary — it comes directly from geometry.

Picture every column of \(A\) as spanning some subspace. Since \(b\) generally doesn’t lie exactly inside that column space (that’s exactly why there’s no exact solution to \(A\delta = b\)), the least squares solution instead projects \(b\) onto the column space — finding the closest point in that space to \(b\).

That leaves an error vector:

\[ r = b - A\delta \]

NoteThe geometric picture

At the optimal \(\delta\), the error vector \(r\) becomes perpendicular (normal) to the column space of \(A\). That perpendicularity condition is exactly what the normal equations enforce — which is where they get their name.

9 The Information Matrix

We define:

\[ \Lambda = A^T A \]

where \(\Lambda\) is called the information matrix, also known as the Hessian of the cost function.

\(A^T A\) has two properties that matter a great deal for how we solve the system:

Property Statement Why it matters
Symmetric \(\Lambda^T = \Lambda\) halves the storage and work needed to represent \(\Lambda\)
Positive semi-definite \(x^T A^T A x \geq 0\) for all \(x\) guarantees the quadratic cost has a well-defined minimum, not a saddle point

That positive semi-definiteness is what makes the system well-behaved enough to allow Cholesky factorization — a fast, numerically stable way to solve the normal equations.

WarningApproximation, not the true Hessian

The true Hessian of the cost function is \(\dfrac{\partial^2 J}{\partial x^2}\), but in practice we approximate it with \(A^T A\).

This approximation is exactly what separates Gauss-Newton from full Newton’s method: Gauss-Newton never computes second derivatives directly. That saves a lot of computation, at the cost of \(A^T A\) only being a good approximation of the true Hessian near the solution.
